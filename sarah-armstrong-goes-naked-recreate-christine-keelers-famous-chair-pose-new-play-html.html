Sarah Armstrong goes naked to recreate Christine Keeler's famous chair pose in new play about former model's notorious sexual liaisons

She admitted that she was feeling more than a little nervous about going naked in a new play about former model Christine Keeler's notorious sexual liaisons.

But in a photocall for the new production Keeler on Friday, actress Sarah Armstrong appeared calm and confident as she recreated the chair pose made famous by Keeler, who caused one of the biggest political scandals of modern times through her sexual liaisons 50 years ago with Tory Minister John Profumo and a Russian military attache based in London.

The new play is based on Keeler's 2001 autobiography The Truth At Last, and was created with the blessing of Keeler herself, who also offered to help Sarah with the role.

Stripping off: Sarah Armstrong goes naked as she takes on the role of Christine Keeler in new play Keeler

Speaking recently about her naked scene in the show, which opens on Friday night at London's Charing Cross Theatre, Sarah, 29, said: 'It’s one thing doing it in a film, another to do it in front of a live audience. I am nervous but excited too.

Christine Keeler (pictured in 1964) was at the centre of the Profumo scandal

‘Christine Keeler is such an iconic character. There’s a lot of pressure but I hope she’ll be pleased with the end result. This is her story, her version of what happened and  we all want to do it justice.’

And Ms Keeler, now 71, added: ‘I’m looking forward to working with Sarah and doing all I can to help her in the play.

‘Sarah is a stunning-looking girl and I’m sure her performance will help people to understand what really went on.

'We were young girls who got caught up in something that overtook us, overwhelmed us. I’m very happy that so many people will get a chance to see the play and hear the truth.’

In an interview last year to publicise her latest book about the affair that rocked the British Establishment, she said: ‘My children don’t want to be associated with that bloody whore Christine Keeler. It’s awful but that’s the way it is.’

The scandal happened at the height of the Cold War when it was discovered that Keeler had been sleeping with both Profumo, the then Conservative Minister for War, and Yevgeny Ivanov, a naval attache based at the Russian Embassy in London.

Keeler and Profumo began their affair after being introduced at a party at the Cliveden estate in 1961 by their mutual friend Stephen Ward, a high-society osteopath and portrait-painter.

Puckering up: One scene also shows Sarah kissing Michael Good, who takes on the role of John Profumo in the production

No holds barred: Keeler and Profumo began their affair after being introduced at a party in 1961

Profumo, who was married to actress Valerie Hobson, had no idea that Keeler was also sleeping with Ivanov.

In March 1963 he told the House of Commons that rumours of his affair were untrue, but  he was forced to resign three months later after admitting he had lied.

Ward, who was prosecuted for living off immoral earnings, took an overdose the day before his trail ended and died on August 3, 1963. Keeler was found guilty of unrelated perjury charges and was sentenced to nine months in prison.

Taking a bow: The pair pose up after the photocall for the production

While Keeler offered her support to the new production at Charing Cross Theatre, directed by actor Paul Nicholas, she is understood to have refused to lend her support to the other production – an Andrew Lloyd Webber musical named Stephen Ward after the osteopath ‘fall guy’ of the affair.

Lord Lloyd-Webber was instead guided by Mandy Rice-Davies, the other woman at the heart of one of the most infamous scandals to rock the establishment.

In that show Ms Keeler is played by Charlotte Spencer who had a small part in the film version of Les Misérables.
